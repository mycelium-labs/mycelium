Here's a practical playbook for a solo founder, broken into phases. I'll keep it concrete since you're already building (Mycelium looks like a dev-tool/SDK play, so I'll lean examples that way).

## Phase 0 — Problem & Scope (1–2 weeks, not months)

1. **Write the problem statement in one sentence.** Who hurts, when, and how badly. ("Agent pipelines silently corrupt context and devs find out in prod.")
2. **Pick ONE user persona.** Not "developers" — something like "engineers running LangGraph/CrewAI agents in production."
3. **Evidence collection.** 10–20 real instances of the problem (GitHub issues, Discord threads, your own incidents — you already have an `incidents/` corpus pattern for this).
4. **Define what you're NOT building.** Scope cuts matter more than scope. Write them down.
5. **Decide your wedge.** The single narrowest thing you can be the best at (e.g., one failure mode, not nine).

## Phase 1 — Validation (before/during MVP)

1. Talk to 10–15 people who have the problem. Don't pitch — ask about their last incident, what they did, what it cost them.
2. Look for the "hair on fire" signal: did they build an internal hack? Are they paying for a workaround?
3. Kill criteria: if nobody describes the problem unprompted, change the problem, not the pitch.

## Phase 2 — MVP (v0.1, 2–6 weeks)

1. **One persona, one problem, one workflow.** For an SDK: install → one decorator/wrapper → visible value in <10 minutes.
2. Build only the happy path plus the failure mode you're protecting against.
3. **Proof artifact:** a reproducible demo showing the failure happening *without* you and prevented *with* you. This is your best sales asset.
4. README + quickstart is part of the MVP, not an afterthought.
5. Instrument it — even crude telemetry/opt-in counters so you know if anyone actually runs it.

## Phase 3 — First users (v0.x, "do things that don't scale")

1. Hand-deliver to 5–10 design partners. Onboard them personally, on a call if needed.
2. Weekly check-ins. Fix their bugs same-day. You're trading white-glove service for brutal feedback.
3. Track one metric: **retention/usage**, not signups. Do they still use it in week 3?
4. Iterate the API surface now, while breaking changes are cheap.

## Phase 4 — GTM (v1.0)

1. **Positioning doc:** one-liner, who it's for, the alternative ("duct tape and prayer"), why now.
2. Pick ONE channel and go deep:
   - Dev tools: open source + content (write up real incidents/postmortems — your incident corpus is literally content), HN/Reddit/X launches, Discord communities.
   - Avoid spreading across 5 channels at 20% effort each.
3. Launch sequence: ship v1 → launch post → Show HN → follow up with every commenter.
4. Define your conversion path early even if monetization comes later: OSS → hosted/cloud, or free tier → team tier.

## Phase 5 — Monetization & repeatability (v1.x–v2)

1. Charge before you think you're ready. First 3 paying customers can be hand-negotiated, weird deals.
2. Price on value (incidents prevented, hours saved), not cost.
3. Find the repeatable motion: where did your best users come from? Do 10x more of that one thing.
4. Only now: dashboards, teams/SSO, enterprise features — pulled by paying customers, not pushed by roadmap fantasy.

## Phase 6 — Scale decisions (v2+)

1. Decide: lifestyle business, raise, or sell. Each changes what you build next.
2. First hire only when something is repeatably working and you're the bottleneck.

## Solo-founder operating rules (all phases)

- **Timebox everything.** Solo means no one stops you from polishing v0 for 6 months.
- 50/50 rule after MVP: half your time building, half talking to users/distributing. Solo founders almost always over-build and under-distribute.
- Keep a public changelog/log — it compounds into marketing (you have a `LOG.md` habit already, that's the right instinct).
- One metric per phase. Phase 2: time-to-value. Phase 3: retention. Phase 4: weekly actives. Phase 5: revenue.

The most common failure sequence is: skip Phase 1, over-build Phase 2, then discover in Phase 4 that nobody wanted it. The phases are cheap insurance against that.
